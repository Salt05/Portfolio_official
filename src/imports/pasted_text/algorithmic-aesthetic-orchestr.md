Platforms utilizing advanced generative models—specifically those leveraging architectures like the Model Context Protocol (MCP) to bridge foundational Large Language Models (LLMs) with native design canvases—have transformed the contemporary designer's role from a purely visual operator to a semantic orchestrator. Tools operating within this paradigm, such as Figma Make, rely on complex, highly sensitive interactions between user-defined text prompts, attached visual contexts, and underlying system guidelines to generate functional prototypes, auto-layouts, and application architectures.However, the efficacy of these generative design models is highly contingent upon the syntactical structure, specificity, and technical constraints embedded within the operator's prompt. Unlike conversational agents designed for open-ended discourse or creative writing, design-oriented LLMs require explicit, highly bounded directives that function similarly to a technical Product Requirements Document (PRD) combined with an artistic manifesto. Ambiguity in prompt construction frequently results in generic, unstructured outputs, or "hallucinated" design components that fail to adhere to responsive principles or modern aesthetic standards. To exact a premium, agency-level aesthetic—particularly one requiring cinematic storytelling, sophisticated visual layering, and nuanced spatial-temporal mechanics—the prompt architecture must heavily constrain the model's output parameters while simultaneously providing rich semantic descriptors for mood, spatial relationship, and narrative flow.The analysis presented herein constructs an exhaustive, multi-disciplinary operational framework for directing an AI design tool to generate a cinematic scroll-based portfolio website. The target subject is an AI Creative Director and Prompt Engineer based in Ho Chi Minh City, a geographic and professional context that implies a high degree of technological fluency intersecting with rapid, innovative creative synthesis. The illusion of a camera moving through three-dimensional space—commonly referred to as parallax depth—requires instructing the design tool to apply differential movement speeds to foreground, midground, and background layers.The mathematical relationship governing a linear parallax effect in digital environments can be defined as:$$Y_{element} = Y_{initial} - (S_{viewport} \times P_{coefficient})$$Where $Y_{element}$ represents the calculated vertical position of the specific layer, $S_{viewport}$ is the total number of pixels the user has scrolled from the top of the document, and $P_{coefficient}$ is the parallax intensity factor assigned to that layer. For instance, a background element might possess a $P_{coefficient}$ of 0.2, moving slowly to simulate distance, while a foreground text element might possess a $P_{coefficient}$ of 0.8 or 1.0, moving rapidly to simulate proximity.While AI design tools like Figma Make may not accept raw mathematical functions in the base prompt text, translating this underlying logic into semantic, tool-specific instructions is paramount. Phrases such as "apply a 100ms delayed stacking effect," "execute a fade-and-rise reveal," or "implement scroll-triggered spatial staggering" ensure the generated code or prototype configuration incorporates the necessary transform: translateY() and opacity transition properties required for fluid motion.
